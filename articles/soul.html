Wherein I get to the point

Products have a soul. Naturally, I don't mean this in a metaphysical way. I mean it in a practical way.

Products reflect the attitudes and values of those that create them.

Sometimes those values are obvious, sometimes not. Sometimes the values are intentional and sometimes they are subconscious. But they‚Äôre always there.

As we‚Äôve built Filthy Rich, I‚Äôve come to a realization. Adam and I always design and build things with solid values or principles in mind. But we rarely articulate them and refine them the way we have with this app.

In writing this article, I‚Äôve realized just how much my latest thinking about UX has influenced my design choices. I‚Äôve also realized how great it is that after many years of doing this, there‚Äôs always more to learn.

The following are the core values that drive the development of Filthy Rich, in no particular order.

  1. It‚Äôs important to design opinionated software. Making your software so the user can configure it how they want is usually a mistake. Instead, put in the time to experiment with different approaches. Find the design that works best for most people.
  2. Keep removing elements from your design until you have only the most essential. You‚Äôll be surprised by what you can do without.
  3. The first principle, specific to Filthy Rich is that you can‚Äôt automate budgeting. The act of recording what you spend is the magic key to helping you get a handle on your spending. There are apps that connect to your bank account and show you what you‚Äôve spent. But automatic tracking isn‚Äôt as powerful for changing behavior.
  4. Manual tracking is critical. So it‚Äôs also critical to make the tracking process as easy and painless as possible. This is one of the main reasons we‚Äôve removed so much noise from the app. If we remove any barriers to manual tracking, then it can become a habit.
  5. User testing is absolutely essential.
